Track 1 — the IsoLG susceptibility score, symbol by symbol

SLIDE 1

The one equation we'll unpack

Why this track exists — the direct finding it's built on — then show the whole equation once.

Bloodworth et al. 2024 (J Clin Invest)

"IsoLG-adducted candidate peptides... are recognized by CD8+ T cells... and prime hypertension" — 4 specific IsoLG-adducted self-peptides, from kidney-overexpressed proteins, were experimentally validated as disease-relevant neoantigens in hypertensive mice.

sIsoLGij = wsite·maxk(ssitejk) + wexpr·sexprj + woverlap·soverlapij,k* + wautoAb·sautoAbj

What it answers

"How much should biology bump this antibody–antigen pair up the priority list — on top of the binding score?"

Analogy

It's a bonus-points rule. The binding model gives the base score; this adds bonus points when the biology looks right for hypertension.

Notation: maxk applies only to ssite — the term that varies with lysine k. sexpr, soverlap, and sautoAb are added directly, since they are constants with respect to k. k* denotes the specific lysine selected by the ssite max — if a downstream tool (e.g. docking) evaluates more than one candidate site, the top-N chemistry sites are evaluated, not just k*.

Say: "Bloodworth gave us 4 experimentally validated adduction sites — that's the evidence base. Four ingredients turn it into a score — a site, a tissue, an overlap, and an autoantibody-target flag. I'll take one per slide, then show the one honest caveat."
SLIDE 2

i, j, k — what the subscripts count

Define the index letters once, before any term uses them.
i → antibody  ·  j → antigen  ·  k → one lysine on antigen j

Plain meaning

Every score is about something — these three letters say what. i picks which antibody, j picks which antigen, and k picks which lysine on that antigen (an antigen has many).

Analogy

Think of a spreadsheet: i is the row (antibody), j is the column (antigen), and k is a specific cell inside that column — one lysine among many on that antigen.

⚠ Caveat (IsoLG) — the 9-mer is a fragment, not the antigen. Why we still build on it.

Two separate events

1. Adduction happens on the whole folded protein — IsoLG glues to a lysine on full-length SGLT2 etc.

2. Processing — the cell's proteasome later shreds that protein into 8–10 aa peptides to show T cells on MHC-I. The Bloodworth 9-mers (e.g. LAGKNLTHI) are these fragments.

Analogy

The graffiti (adduct) is sprayed on a whole wall. Later, a photographer crops a 9-letter close-up to file a report. The crop isn't the wall — it's evidence about one spot on it.

The 9-mer (Bloodworth)What an antibody sees
Exists asa chopped fragment in an MHC groovethe whole folded protein, unchopped
Made forCD8+ T-cell inspectionnothing — antibody binds the native surface

So why use the 9-mer at all? Only as a sequence signature of a lysine known to get adducted — the "motif" term. We borrow the chemistry around that lysine, not a claim that antibodies see 9-mers. That keeps us honest about T-cell vs B-cell recognition.

Why we still pursue this track despite the mismatch: the adduction chemistry (which lysines get modified, on which proteins) is real and validated, independent of which immune cell later reads it. We're borrowing only that chemistry layer for ssite/sexpr — the antibody-binding judgement is never delegated to Bloodworth's T-cell data, it stays entirely with the ranker. This is a bounded, falsifiable use of the evidence, not an overreach.

SLIDE 3

ssitejk — is this lysine attackable?

The chemistry term. One lysine k on antigen j. Directly inspired by Bloodworth's screen.
ssitejk

Plain meaning

IsoLG (from oxidative stress) glues itself onto lysine (K) residues — but only lysines that are exposed on the surface, chemically reactive, and resemble a known adduction site. This score rates one lysine on all three.

Analogy

A door can only be graffitied if it (1) faces the street, (2) has paint that sticks, and (3) looks like doors that have been tagged before. A buried, non-stick, or unfamiliar-looking door is safe.

  • ① Exposure — how: run NetSurfP-3.0 on the antigen sequence → per-residue RSA in [0,1] for every lysine. Pexposed(k) = RSA(k). A separate tool call, not a by-product of ESM-C embeddings.
    Feasible at 20k? Yes — free for academic use (institutional registration, no cost), GPU-accelerated, ~30–35 min for 20k sequences on comparable hardware (published benchmark: ~100s/1,000 seqs). Run the local standalone version, not the web server — the web server caps jobs at 10,000 sequences.
  • ② Sequence-context heuristic: in the ±3–5 window around k, count nearby K/R vs D/E using a simple charge-table lookup, as a rough proxy for local pKa. This is an unvalidated heuristic, not a measured chemistry property — real local pKa/reactivity depends on 3D electrostatics, solvent environment, and microenvironment effects that a flat sequence-window count cannot capture. It carries a modest weight relative to exposure and motif, and rankings are checked with this term removed entirely to confirm results are robust without it.
    Underlying intuition (still a heuristic, not a validated rule): lysine's tail can be protonated (+charged) or neutral — IsoLG can only attach to the neutral form, and pKa shifts that balance. Nearby K/R (positive) neighbours are assumed to raise local pKa (less reactive); nearby D/E (negative) neighbours to lower it (more reactive) — a first-order approximation only, not confirmed by structural calculation.
  • ③ Motif — how (this is the Bloodworth-inspired part): slide a 9-residue window across the entire antigen (not just centred on k) and score every window's similarity — BLOSUM62 (default) or ESM-C cosine — against each of the 4 validated peptides (LAGKNLTHI, YILKLPLPL, GSPKQHEVV, MQLPSKVVL). Take the best-matching window overall; if it contains a lysine, that k's motif score is the alignment similarity.
    Feasible at 20k? Yes, with BLOSUM62 as default — a lookup-table sum per window, tens of millions of comparisons total across 20k antigens, seconds–minutes to run. ESM-C cosine similarity is feasible only if pooled from the per-residue embeddings we already compute for the ranker — re-embedding every 9-mer window from scratch would be far too slow.
    Getting an embedding for the 4 Bloodworth 9-mers: none exists pre-made — run ESM-C once per peptide, treating each 9-mer as its own tiny standalone sequence (e.g. just "LAGKNLTHI" alone, no surrounding protein), then average its 9 per-residue output vectors into one reference vector. A one-time, negligible cost (4 short sequences), not repeated per antigen.
    Context mismatch caveat: the 4 reference vectors are embedded in isolation (no surrounding protein), but each antigen window's vector is pooled from residues embedded in their full-protein context — the two sides aren't perfectly like-for-like, which is a real (if usually minor) source of noise. This asymmetry doesn't exist for BLOSUM62 (pure letter-matching either side) — one more reason to prefer it as the default, with ESM-C cosine as an optional, imperfect refinement.
Say: "This is pure chemistry plus one disease-specific signal — sequence in, an off-the-shelf accessibility predictor, and a similarity check against Bloodworth's 4 validated sites."
SLIDE 4

maxk — why we take the best lysine

maxk applies only to ssite — sexpr and sautoAb don't vary with k, so they sit outside the max.
maxk ∈ 𝒦j ( ssitejk )

Plain meaning

An antigen has many lysines (the set 𝒦j). We don't average them — we take the single most susceptible one, and call it k*. One good adduction site is enough to make the antigen disease-relevant.

Analogy

A house is a break-in risk if it has one unlocked window — we don't average over all the locked ones.

  • Scope: maxk wraps only ssite, not the whole formula. sexpr and sautoAb are constants with respect to k (they describe the antigen, not a specific lysine), so they are added directly in the master equation (Slide 1), not inside a max over k.
  • k* and soverlap: the lysine selected by this max, k*, is what soverlapij,k* checks the antibody's footprint against (Slide 6). Where a downstream structural tool (docking) evaluates more than one candidate site rather than only k*, that scope is stated explicitly — e.g. "the top-N chemistry sites are evaluated."
  • Why max, not sum/average over all lysines? Bloodworth's computational screen identified positions 4, 6, 7 (within a 9-mer) as generally favourable for adduction — among the 4 individually validated peptides, K sits at position 4 in three and position 6 in the fourth. The signal concentrates at a few specific positions, not spread evenly. Summing would also bias by protein size: a large antigen with many mediocre lysines could outscore a small antigen with one perfectly exposed one, purely from residue count.
  • Considered alternative: top-2/top-3 average, as a hedge against one noisy lysine score dominating — not adopted by default, but worth flagging as a sensitivity check.
Say: "max_k applies only to s^site — s^expr and s^autoAb are antigen-level constants, so they sit outside the max. Averaging over lysines would punish big proteins for having many safe ones; max asks the right question: is there at least one vulnerable site."
SLIDE 5

sexprj — is the antigen even in the right place?

The tissue term. Depends only on antigen j, from Tabula Muris Senis. Not re-deriving the 3-organ filter — that's already applied. This asks two graded questions on top of it.
sexprj

Plain meaning — two questions, not one

① Magnitude: among antigens that pass the organ/DC filter, how strongly is it expressed there — not just present/absent. ② Age trend: does its expression rise with age, matching the known pattern that hypertension and oxidative/IsoLG burden accumulate with age? TMS uniquely gives us the old-vs-young comparison to check this — a single-snapshot dataset like HPA can't.

Direct quotes (Rodriguez-Iturbe 2017, Physiol Rev)

On age-accumulation: SHR "are normotensive 3–4 wk after birth and develop progressive hypertension as they age." And for a named autoantigen: "SHR are normotensive at 4 wk of age and have overexpression of HSP72. At 40 wk of age, HSP70 and HSP72 are overexpressed in the SHR" — a disease-relevant self-protein's expression measurably rising with age in the same model.

  • How: pull normalised expression (log-CPM) for antigen j from the TMS dataset, subset to {kidney, aorta, heart} × {dendritic cell, macrophage}. Take the max across that subset for the magnitude signal — one strongly-expressing tissue is enough, the same logic used for lysine selection in ssite.
  • Age term — method: TMS is normal ageing in mice, not hypertensive tissue, so a naive log(meanold / meanyoung) or a max-across-cells is too weak a proxy — a raw mean ratio can reflect cell-composition shifts, sex differences, or ordinary ageing rather than hypertensive oxidative stress. We compute donor-level pseudobulk differential expression (aggregate counts per donor/replicate before comparing, not per-cell), report with a confidence interval and FDR-corrected significance, and treat age-association as a signal only when it clears that bar. TMS is an appropriate tissue/age context source, with this caveat kept visible wherever the age term is used.
  • Upweight expression in kidney / aorta / heart — every rodent model converges on these organs
  • Upweight dendritic-cell / immune expression — where IsoLG adducts actually form
Say: "We've already filtered by organ — this is asking two more graded questions: how strongly is it expressed there, and does that expression rise with age. The age signal uses donor-level pseudobulk with FDR correction rather than a raw mean ratio, because TMS is normal ageing, not hypertensive tissue."
SLIDE 6

soverlapij,k* — does the antibody reach the site? (verify-tier only)

The only term that involves the antibody i. Per-pair, recomputed for every (i, j) — but only ever populated for the shortlist, by design (see caveat below).
soverlapij,k*

Plain meaning

For a specific antibody i and antigen j: does i's predicted binding footprint fall near the susceptible lysine k* selected by maxk(ssite) on Slide 4?

Analogy

The unlocked window matters more if the burglar is already standing next to it, not on the other side of the house.

  • How (verify tier, shortlist only): AF3/docking — score 1 if k* is within ~8Å of any antibody CDR residue in the modelled complex, else 0. Where docking evaluates multiple candidate sites rather than only k*, that scope is stated explicitly (e.g. "top-3 chemistry sites checked").
  • Excludes the ranker's own cross-attention, and any rescue tier's own binding score (e.g. MINT's s(1)). Both are already baked into the score soverlap would be added to — reusing them here would double-count the same evidence.
  • Neutral by default across the full 20k pool (AF3/docking only runs on the shortlist); still only predicted, not solved, structure — treated as evidence, not conclusion.
Say: "Sourced only from AF3/docking, not the ranker's own attention — that would double-count evidence already in the binding score. Verify-tier only, adds confidence, never overrides a weak binder."
SLIDE 7

w• and αIsoLG — the volume knobs

sIsoLG is the biology score for one pair. It plugs into the ranker's tier score s(r) — r = 0 is the primary ranker, r = 1...R are the rescue tiers from Phase 3.
s(r)+IsoLGij = s(r)ij + αIsoLG · sIsoLGij

Plain meaning

wsite, wexpr, woverlap, wautoAb (inside sIsoLG) balance the four ingredients against each other. αIsoLG is separate — it sets how loud the whole biology score is versus the binding score s(r) that's already been computed by the ranker/rescue tiers.

Analogy

A graphic-equaliser: the w's balance the bands (site vs tissue vs overlap vs autoantibody-flag), α is the master volume for the whole biology channel versus the binding channel. We tune both, we don't let the model learn them.

  • What AbRank can actually check — a safety check, not a hypertension-relevance check: AbRank has no hypertension-specific labels (no IsoLG tags, no disease-tissue-relevance tags) — it's a generic binding-affinity benchmark. So AbRank cannot tell us whether wsite=0.6 is "biologically correct." What it can check is narrower: does adding αIsoLG·sIsoLG on top of s(r) preserve AbRank's known-pair ranking accuracy (pairwise accuracy, top-K recovery), or does it corrupt it? That's a stability check, bounding α from above — not a validation of the w's themselves.
  • The actual weighting — to be discussed: since no benchmark has hypertension ground truth, the relative importance of chemistry vs tissue vs antibody-overlap vs the curated autoantibody-target flag comes from sensitivity analysis plus supervisor judgment on the resulting biology-prioritised watchlist (Phase 4) — not from an AbRank score. Example numbers are a starting point to react to in discussion, not a result we can claim is benchmark-validated.
  • What biology can and cannot do to the final ranking: because α is bounded, a pair with a strong s(r) stays high regardless of its biology score — biology only adds, it never gates or multiplies. So the score is genuinely useful as supplementary re-ranking among already-strong binders (a tie-breaker toward hypertension-relevant candidates). It is not designed to work as a rescue mechanism that promotes a weak binder with a striking biology score into the shortlist — if it did that, α would be tuned too large, which is a failure mode, not a feature.
  • Where "rescue by biology" actually happens instead: a weak-binder/high-biology pair is exactly what the IsoLG biology-prioritised watchlist (Phase 4) is for — a separate, human-reviewed side list, not an automatic score boost inside the ranking formula.
Say: "AbRank only checks that adding biology doesn't break ranking on known pairs — it can't validate hypertension relevance, since it has no hypertension labels. The w's themselves are set with supervisors through sensitivity analysis. And by design, biology only re-ranks among strong binders — it never rescues a weak one; that's what the separate watchlist is for."

Track 2 — hypertension-associated autoantibody targets across models/species

SLIDE 1

Why we need a B-cell anchor

Motivation before any symbol — the honest gap in the IsoLG evidence.
antibody ≠ T-cell evidence

The gap

Bloodworth's peptides prove T-cell recognition. But SABE studies antibodies (B-cell). We need at least one piece of evidence on the antibody side of hypertension.

The find

Jama et al., 2022 (online 2021): autoantibodies develop in SHR (spontaneously hypertensive rats) — evidence of altered humoral autoimmunity, an antibody-side association.

Say: "This gives an antibody-side association — not proof antibodies cause hypertension. It lets us argue disease relevance without the T-cell caveat, while being careful not to overstate causality."
SLIDE 2

What "autoantibody" means, plainly

Define the term before using it as evidence.
auto + antibody

Plain meaning

An antibody that binds the body's own proteins ("auto" = self). In SHR, the immune system makes antibodies against self-tissue — evidence of altered humoral autoimmunity, not (on its own) proof that those antibodies cause hypertension. Establishing causation would need transfer or blockade experiments, which this evidence doesn't provide.

Analogy

A guard dog that starts biting the household it's meant to protect. The bites (autoantibodies) show something has changed internally — they don't, by themselves, prove the bites are what's making the house sick.

Say: "Autoantibodies in SHR are evidence of altered humoral autoimmunity — an antibody-side association with hypertension, not proof of causation. That's still useful: it tells us antibodies against self-antigens are part of the picture."

⚠ Caveat — no named targets in Jama et al. 2022. Why we still keep this track.

The actual problem

Jama et al., 2022 (online 2021) is a review, citing Wallukat 1995: it reports that autoantibodies exist in SHR (a rat, Rattus norvegicus model) — it does not name the target proteins, sequences, or epitopes. We cannot build a lookup table with zero named entries. The "SHR target" framing from Slide 1 has no concrete gene to check against TMS.

Analogy

A news article saying "burglaries happened here" but never naming which houses. Useful atmosphere, useless as an actual watch-list.

Why we still pursue this track: other literature does name concrete autoantibody targets — but they come from a mix of species and models, not from SHR itself. We use them as translational positive controls, each labelled with its own species/model/evidence grade, not as an SHR-specific target set:

They are reasonable translational positive controls precisely because the underlying autoantibody phenomenon is well-documented in hypertension-relevant human/cardiovascular contexts, not because they were derived from SHR.

SLIDE 3

sautoAbj — a curated-prior evidence flag, not a quantitative binding score

Loop alignment confirms orthology/mapping — it does not, by itself, show that j is actually autoantibody-targeted. QC gates and evidence weighting are two different things.
sautoAbj = QC(j) · evidence_grade(species match, model match, direct evidence)

What this score can and cannot claim

Cannot claim: that loop-sequence identity between mouse j and a human epitope shows j is autoantibody-targeted in mice — high identity only shows the mouse gene is the right ortholog, which is an orthology/mapping fact, not evidence of targeting. Can claim: a curated prior — antigen j corresponds to a protein with documented autoantibody evidence somewhere in the hypertension/cardiovascular literature, graded by how directly that evidence applies to our setting.

Analogy

Confirming a mouse gene is "the same receptor" as AT1R is like confirming two buildings have the same street address in different cities — it tells us we're looking at the right building, not that a burglary happened there. The evidence grade is the burglary report; loop identity is just the address check.

  • An evidence flag, not a similarity score: for each of the ~5 named proteins, a curated grade combines (a) species match (is the direct evidence from mouse, rat, or human?), (b) disease-model match (hypertension-specific, or general cardiovascular?), and (c) directness of evidence (functional autoantibody with named epitope, vs. elevated titres, vs. review-level mention only). This is a small, manually curated table — not a formula computed from sequence.
  • Loop identity and exposure are QC criteria, not evidence: before assigning any grade to antigen j, we check (i) j is confirmed the correct mouse ortholog via pairwise alignment (Needleman-Wunsch/Smith-Waterman, percent identity ≥80–90% against the known human loop) and (ii) the matching loop is surface-exposed. Failing either QC check disqualifies the target regardless of how strong the literature evidence is — but passing them does not itself generate evidence, it only confirms the right protein is being scored.
  • Exposure source for GPCRs — curated topology, not NetSurfP-3.0: AT1R/α1-AR/β1-AR are GPCRs with published, curated transmembrane topology (UniProt annotates exactly which residue ranges are extracellular vs. membrane-embedded vs. intracellular, from solved structures and validated predictions). That annotation confirms the loop is extracellular. A general sequence-based RSA predictor like NetSurfP-3.0 is not reliable for this question — it estimates local solvent accessibility from sequence alone and isn't designed to resolve membrane-topology questions (extracellular vs. cytoplasmic loop), so it could misclassify a loop's true orientation. NetSurfP-3.0 is appropriate for ssite's soluble-protein lysines (Slide 3, Track 1); GPCR loop topology relies on curated annotation instead.
  • Identity gate: is antigen j one of the ~5 named proteins? If not, sautoAbj = 0 — no fallback search is run, since there is no established general rule that loop-mimicry alone implies targeting.
  • Named targets and mouse gene IDs: AT1R → Agtr1a/Agtr1b, α1-AR → Adra1a, β1-AR → Adrb1, HSP70/HSP72 → Hspa1a/Hspa1b.
  • How it plugs into the master equation: a fourth, antigen-level term added alongside the others — sIsoLGij = wsite·maxk(ssitejk) + wexpr·sexprj + woverlap·soverlapij,k* + wautoAb·sautoAbj — same bounded, sensitivity-tuned weight, never a separate override. (See Slide 7 for why maxk now wraps only the site term.)
Say: "s^autoAb is a curated evidence flag, not a computed similarity score. Loop alignment is a QC step confirming we've got the right mouse gene — the actual evidence grade comes from a small, manually curated table of species/model/directness, reviewed with supervisors, not from sequence comparison alone."
